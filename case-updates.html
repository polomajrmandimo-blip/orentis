Supreme Court of Appeal 14 March 2026

Meridian Capital (Pty) Ltd v Stellargate Fund Managers (Pty) Ltd

Holding: A fund manager's duty of care to investors is not extinguished by the terms of a limited partnership agreement where those terms conflict with the manager's statutory obligations under the FAIS Act.

In this significant decision, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that a limited partnership agreement cannot be used to contractually override a financial services provider's statutory duties under the Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act 37 of 2002. The fund manager had sought to rely on a broad exclusion clause in the partnership agreement to defeat claims brought by limited partners following a series of unauthorised asset disposals. The SCA found that the exclusion was unenforceable to the extent that it purported to permit conduct that would otherwise constitute a breach of the general duty of care imposed by the FAIS Act and the applicable conduct standard. For asset managers and fund administrators, the practical consequence is clear: statutory compliance obligations are a floor, not a ceiling, and no contractual arrangement — however carefully drafted — can displace them.

Gauteng Division, Johannesburg 29 January 2026

FirstRand Bank Limited v Cornerstone Developments (Pty) Ltd & Others

Holding: A cession in securitatem debiti of future book debts remains effective against the insolvent estate of the cedent where registration requirements have been substantially, if not formally, complied with.

The Gauteng High Court considered the enforceability of a general cession of book debts against the liquidator of an insolvent company, in circumstances where the cession had not been registered in strict accordance with the Insolvency Act formalities. The court found, applying a purposive construction of section 53(1)(b) of the Insolvency Act, that the registration requirement is directory rather than peremptory in cases where the creditor bank had taken all commercially reasonable steps to perfect its security. The judgment provides important guidance for banking and structured finance transactions involving the cession of receivables as collateral, and is likely to be relied upon by lenders seeking to enforce security over book debts in insolvency proceedings.
